Turn every bowl into a product decision.

Candidate thesis
Every release should make Bowl Builder easier to trust, easier to operate, and easier to scale.

The actual mandate

Make a promising robotic makeline repeatable across recipes, sites, operators, support models, and economics by keeping food behavior, electromechanical performance, order truth, operator flow, serviceability, and commercial evidence inside one product decision system.

Company moment — public context

Connected system

Orders, dispensing, cameras, digital bowl records, robot coordination, and packaging create an end-to-end evidence chain.

Restaurant reality

Recipes, ingredients, environments, operators, cleaning, maintenance, and support shape whether technical performance becomes customer value.

Scale question

Every pilot must establish what generalizes, what remains contextual, and what earns the next deployment.

PM opportunity

Give the team a shared product contract, decision record, evidence view, and learning loop without adding bureaucracy.

Central operating tensions

Speed vs. integrated readinessMove quickly without discovering cross-functional requirements only at launch.
Modularity vs. system coherenceAllow product evolution while keeping recipes, hardware, software, operators, and economics aligned.
Customer specificity vs. reuseRespect site context while extracting product patterns that compound.
Throughput vs. recoverabilityProtect pace while making exceptions, intervention, and service learning visible.
Technical proof vs. business proofShow that a release works and that it deserves broader adoption.

Executive interview questions

  1. Which product decision is currently hardest because evidence lives across food, hardware, software, field operations, and economics?
  2. What must an external pilot prove before Lab37 considers the learning reusable rather than site-specific?
  3. Which Bowl Builder performance signals are already trusted, and where does the team still depend on manual interpretation?
  4. How are culinary requirements, machine acceptance, and customer outcomes reconciled when a trade-off is time-sensitive?
  5. Where does the product currently carry the most change burden for operators, support, manufacturing, or customers?
  6. How does the team decide whether an exception becomes a roadmap item, an operating standard, or a bounded customer configuration?
  7. Twelve months from now, what would make this product manager indispensable to engineering, customers, and commercial leadership?

Closing position

My product-management value is making complex work visible enough to decide. I connect customer and field truth to the requirements, evidence, ownership, launch discipline, and operating cadence that let multidisciplinary teams move faster together.
