Bowl Builder is not simply a robot. It is a complete product system for turning an order into food that is accurate, timely, traceable, operable, and economically repeatable.

That is why the Robotics Product Manager opportunity feels so aligned with the work I enjoy most. The product has to reconcile mechanical design, software, ingredient behavior, telemetry, operator workflow, manufacturing, support, customer experience, and unit economics in the real world. My career has repeatedly placed me at those intersections, translating field reality into product and operating decisions.

At Vape-Jet, I lead product-facing operating work in a software-enabled robotics and machine-vision environment with approximately 1,000 fielded robots. I connect fleet signals, customer support, non-reported error codes, production, quality, purchasing, software issue flow, Jira, ERP/Odoo, training, and leadership review into clearer triage, acceptance, release-readiness, and learning mechanisms. The work is practical: make the signal visible, identify the decision, clarify ownership, and return evidence to the product.

I also bring launch discipline from Amazon, where I led the launch and execution of Prime Pittsburgh in a 24/7 customer-backward environment supporting roughly five million second-day orders annually. That experience taught me how a customer promise is protected through metrics, staffing models, escalation paths, standard work, and daily operating rhythm. Earlier, at Compunetics, I led engineering, manufacturing, quality, and customer delivery in high-reliability electronics, building fluency across complex assemblies, validation, documentation, lifecycle controls, supplier handoffs, and manufacturability.

For our conversation, I created an independent candidate vision that demonstrates how I would approach Lab37’s mandate. Its central operating artifact—the Bowl Builder Product Contract—connects recipe behavior, electromechanical performance, software truth, operator flow, serviceability, and economics to one release and pilot decision. The companion 90-day plan shows how I would first read the system, prove one complete decision loop, and then make the learning repeatable.

My thesis is simple: every release should make Bowl Builder easier to trust, easier to operate, and easier to scale. I would be excited to discuss where the team needs the most product clarity now—roadmap trade-offs, pilot evidence, KPI visibility, release readiness, or the path from field signal to reusable product decision.
